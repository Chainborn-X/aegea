# Regions

Regions in Aegea should be built as places of exploration first. Each region needs traversal hooks, secrets, strong silhouettes, environmental storytelling, and a reason to wonder what lies just beyond the screen.

## Coastal Regions

The starting emotional language of Aegea.

- Turquoise water, pale stone, warm sand, grasses, shells, driftwood, fishing ruins, and broken coastal shrines.
- Early exploration should show distant islands and inaccessible cliffs to establish scale.
- Paths should split naturally between shorelines, inland slopes, caves, and ruins.
- The sea should feel inviting, ancient, and slightly unknowable.

## Sea Caves

Liminal spaces between sunlight and mystery.

- Blue reflected light, wet stone, echoing chambers, tide pools, mineral deposits, and hidden shrines.
- Entrances may be visible from beaches but require later traversal abilities or timing.
- Use water sound, shafts of light, and glimpses of submerged architecture.

## Forests

Dense but not generic fantasy forests.

- Olive, pine, cypress, laurel, myrtle, wildflowers, dry grasses, broken columns, and old stone paths.
- Forests should alternate between enclosed paths and sudden clearings with vistas.
- Secrets should hide through natural layering rather than arbitrary walls.

## Sacred Groves

Places where nature, myth, and memory overlap.

- Springs, old trees, offerings, carved stones, quiet light, petals, bees, birds, and half-buried statues.
- Combat should be rare or meaningful.
- These spaces can host healing, lore, blessings, or encounters with divine traces.

## Marshlands

Slow, reflective, and slightly dangerous.

- Reeds, shallow water, ruined causeways, fog banks, insects, half-sunken statues, and old boundary stones.
- Navigation should involve finding solid ground, reading landmarks, and discovering raised paths.
- Avoid making marshes visually muddy; retain color, light, and plant life.

## Mountains

Scale, isolation, weather, and ancient paths.

- Switchbacks, cliff roads, mountain flowers, shrines, wind, snow at high elevations, and exposed ruins.
- Mountains should create long-term goals through visible peaks and distant temples.
- Traversal can include ledges, bridges, caves, lifts, and weather gates.

## Ancient Temples

Sacred architecture reclaimed by time.

- Cracked marble, broken roofs, sunbeams, vines, moss, roots, painted fragments, statues, and ceremonial water.
- Temples should rarely be pristine.
- They should feel designed around rituals, alignments, processions, hidden chambers, and divine symbolism.

## Underworld Regions

Mythic descent without grimdark excess.

- Pale stone, still water, dim gold, asphodel fields, obsidian, echoing halls, and quiet spirits.
- The Underworld should feel solemn and strange, not purely evil.
- Hope can appear through lamps, flowers, songs, and memory.

## Forgotten Villages

Traces of ordinary life.

- Empty courtyards, collapsed roofs, bread ovens, wells, woven remnants, painted pottery, overgrown gardens, and personal shrines.
- Villages should humanize the world without becoming crowded hubs too early.
- Environmental storytelling should suggest who lived there and why they left.

## Cliffside Ruins

Vertical wonder and dangerous beauty.

- White stone against sea, broken stairs, hanging vines, arches, gulls, wind, and distant horizons.
- Use parallax and vertical layering to make the player feel small.
- Hidden doors, collapsed bridges, and visible unreachable paths should create curiosity.
